Reflection

Words: 348.

Before this class, I thought sexuality was a simple topic of women and men, but I had never actually stopped to think about how many layers are involved or how much of what I believed was just something society had taught me without me questioning it.

I learned that sexuality has different dimensions and shapes, for example in gender expression, identity, orientation, and the body itself. I also learned that none of these aspects automatically determine the others. The idea that stuck with me the most was that gender is largely a binary social construct because of the rules around it (what is "for girls," what is "for boys," how you are supposed to look or act) are things society invented and keeps reinforcing.

On a personal level, this learning made me look back at assumptions I didn't even know I had. I realized I had been judging things as "normal" or "strange" based on standards I never chose to believe. At first, this realization was uncomfortable, but important as sexuality affects everybody in the world, and people who are a minority can feel easily excluded and have a bad quality of live according to statistics our teacher provided.

This changed how I see my role at Escuela Aurelio Chiriboga because these kids are at an age where they are still figuring out who they are. Therefore, I now notice that the "little jokes" that students make towards classmates (who don't fit a gender role) are not just "jokes" or "innocent moments", those are hurtful comments that will continue in the future. I also learned that it is very important to fix and correct these conducts as soon as possible on children before they transform into hate actions or violence when they grow up.

In conclusion, knowing what is actually behind those dynamics makes me feel more responsible to teach others about the nuances of sexuality and not just ignore them as a "tabu" topic, since it shapes the geopolitical decisions around the world and the way people tolerate or exclude others who are different from themselves. Therefore, sexuality is also connected with discrimination and migration tolerance (which is nowadays very common around the world).

2. My previous definition

Before, I thought that a socially responsible citizen is an action oriented individual who recognizes the systemic roots of social and environmental inequalities and takes proactive steps to address them. Hence, a responsible citizen actively listens to community needs, champions or creates local initiatives, and makes sustainable, conscious lifestyle choices to promote both equitable societal outcomes and environmental stewardship.

3. What I think now

Responsible citizenship means understanding that the problems affecting our community are not isolated, they are connected to structural failures in areas like education, health, and the economy. It is not enough to participate in an organization or volunteer your time.

4. Written comparison

Words: 350.

When I wrote my initial assignment on responsible citizenship, I described it as participating in organizations that tackle social inequalities and being aware of the community around you just like Escuela Aurelio Chiriboga or Huertomanías. I also identified myself as a "Conscientious Citizen" on the Active Citizen Continuum because I knew about problems these organizations wanted to solve and therefore I tried to act on that knowledge. Now looking back, I realize that definition was still mostly about showing up and having the right attitude.

What changed is that I now understand that responsible citizenship requires more than awareness. After actually working at Aurelio Chiriboga, I saw firsthand that the school only partially addresses the problem. It provides food and tutoring, but it doesn't actively push for structural change beyond its walls. I now think a responsible citizen has to care about that gap too, not just the part of the problem that happens inside the organization. That is a shift I could not have made just by reading about it. Therefore, I now think that a responsible citizen actively tries to understand why a problem exists, and uses that understanding to act with intention, and takes responsibility both inside and outside their organization.

5. Three essential values

The values I now see as a key to responsible civic action.

Empathy with structural awareness

Not just feeling bad for someone, but understanding why they are in that situation. This allows you to connect their experience to broader social issues and make people feel seen. Without this, you can be wrong and blame the individual instead of the system.

Intentionality

Showing up is not enough. Every action like how you talk to a student, what you choose to teach, how you handle a difficult moment, should be guided by a clear purpose connected to the bigger problem or structural problem. This is important for avoiding superficial volunteerism and no achieving great results in the community.

Honesty about limitations

A responsible citizen recognizes what their organization can and cannot fix, and does not pretend that individual effort solves structural problems. This is important to make volunteering a group activity and not an individual aproach.

Two Personal Commitments as a Student and Future Professional

The table below summarizes my commitments, how I will evaluate them, and the concrete steps I will take to fulfill them.

Commitment Indicators (How will you evaluate whether the commitment has been fulfilled?) Concrete Actions (What concrete steps will you take to fulfill this commitment? Minimum: 4 actions)
As a student
  • I can point to at least 3 specific situations from my service hours where I connected what I observed to a structural cause discussed in class (poverty cycle, health disparities, discrimination).
  • I complete my remaining service hours with a clear purpose behind each interaction, not just showing up.
  • By the end of the semester, I can explain the root causes of the school's challenges in my own words, without needing notes or theory language.
  1. Before each service session, I will remind myself of one structural concept from class (e.g., poverty cycle, triple approach) and look for evidence of it during my time at the school.
  2. After each visit, I will write a short personal note (even 3–5 sentences) about something I observed that connected to class content, not for a grade, just to keep the reflection habit active.
  3. When I tutor students who are struggling, I will try to find out (naturally, not forcefully) if there is a reason beyond academics, tiredness, hunger, family situations, and adjust how I work with them that day.
  4. I will bring at least one topic not covered in the school's curriculum (emotional regulation, basic digital tools, self-expression) into my sessions with students, at least once a month for the rest of the semester.
As a future professional
  • In my first year of professional work, I maintain at least one active connection to a social organization (through volunteering, donating, or sharing their work).
  • In work situations involving people from different social or economic backgrounds, I can point to specific decisions I made that were guided by empathy and structural awareness.
  • I do not reduce a person's difficulty to personal failure, I actively challenge that kind of thinking in my professional environment when I see it.
  1. Before I graduate, I will research which organizations in my professional field are doing work connected to social inequality in Ecuador and identify at least two I would want to support or collaborate with in the future.
  2. In any professional project or job that involves working with communities, I will ask the question "what is the structural cause behind this?" before proposing any solution, the same way I learned to ask it during PASEC.
  3. I will commit to at least 4 hours per month of continued community involvement (through volunteering, mentoring, or local initiatives), even after PASEC ends, because I now understand that one semester is not enough to create real change.
  4. If I ever have people reporting to me or students depending on me in a professional setting, I will make it a point to create space for them to speak honestly about barriers they are facing, the same way I learned that students at Aurelio Chiriboga needed someone to actually notice them, not just teach them.
